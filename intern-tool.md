# Intern Tool

## Recruit → Onboard → Manage → Capture → Exit

*A streamlined hiring-to-exit system for short-term roles: interns, fellows, seasonal staff, and project-based positions.*

*Operates under the Frozen Kernel. Never inside it.*

*Companion to: Recruiter Tool (full methodology)*

*Population = 1*

🧊

-----

## How This Differs from the Full Recruiter Tool

The Recruiter Tool is a seven-stage system designed for permanent or long-term key hires. Interns are different. The timeline is compressed, the stakes are lower, the evaluation is lighter, and there’s an element the full tool doesn’t address: the intern leaves, and the knowledge must stay.

|                    |Full Recruiter Tool            |Intern Tool                     |
|--------------------|-------------------------------|--------------------------------|
|**Stages**          |7 (Gate through Annual Review) |5 (Screen through Exit)         |
|**Typical duration**|12+ months in role             |8–16 weeks in role              |
|**Interview depth** |Phone + Panel (STAR) + Final   |Single interview (30–45 min)    |
|**Evaluation**      |90-day + annual review         |Midpoint check-in + final eval  |
|**Exit process**    |Not addressed (role continues) |Knowledge capture + presentation|
|**Primary risk**    |Wrong person in a critical role|Knowledge walks out the door    |

|Stage 1           |Stage 2                |Stage 3           |Stage 4           |Stage 5                       |
|------------------|-----------------------|------------------|------------------|------------------------------|
|**Screen**        |**Interview**          |**Onboard**       |**Manage**        |**Exit**                      |
|Application review|30–45 min, single round|Week 1 orientation|Midpoint + ongoing|Knowledge capture             |

## Stage 1: Screen

**Purpose:** Sort applications quickly. Interns are evaluated on potential and fit, not proven track record.

**Who:** Supervisor or hiring manager. One person. This does not need a panel.

**Method:** Gate first (binary pass/fail on logistics), then score on potential, then decide.

### Intern Gate (Pass/Fail)

|Requirement                                               |Pass / Fail    |Notes                      |
|----------------------------------------------------------|---------------|---------------------------|
|Available for the full internship period                  |               |                           |
|Able to work on-site / hybrid as required                 |               |                           |
|Meets any academic or program requirements (if applicable)|               |                           |
|[Organization-specific requirement]                       |               |                           |
|**Gate Result**                                           |**Pass / Fail**|*If FAIL, do not continue.*|

### Intern Potential Scoring

Score on potential and learning orientation, not proven competency. Adjust criteria to match the internship’s focus area.

|      |Criteria                         |Score (1–5)|Evidence                                                                    |
|------|---------------------------------|-----------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|**P1**|Mission Interest & Motivation    |           |Why this org? Why this role? Genuine or generic?                            |
|**P2**|Relevant Skills or Coursework    |           |Related experience, academic work, volunteer history, or transferable skills|
|**P3**|Communication Quality            |           |Cover letter clarity, professionalism, attention to detail                  |
|**P4**|Initiative & Learning Orientation|           |Evidence of self-direction, curiosity, willingness to figure things out     |
|      |**Total**                        |**/20**    |                                                                            |

**Decision:** Score 14–20 = Interview. Score 10–13 = Interview if pool is thin. Below 10 = Decline.

-----

## Stage 2: Interview (30–45 Minutes, Single Round)

**Purpose:** Confirm fit, assess learning orientation, and set mutual expectations. One interview is sufficient for an intern role.

**Who:** Direct supervisor + one other person. Two perspectives, not a panel.

### Interview Structure

|Min |Segment          |Content                                                                                                                                                                |
|----|-----------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|5   |Welcome & Context|Introductions. Explain the org, the role, and the interview structure. Put them at ease — most interns are early-career and nervous.                                   |
|20  |Questions        |5–6 questions mapped to the four potential criteria. See suggested questions below.                                                                                    |
|10  |Role Reality     |Be honest about what the internship involves. Describe a typical week. Mention the less exciting parts. Set expectations about supervision, autonomy, and deliverables.|
|5–10|Their Questions  |Encourage questions. What they ask tells you as much as what they answer.                                                                                              |

### Suggested Interview Questions

|      |Question                                                                                                                 |Listen For                                                                                                 |
|------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|**P1**|What specifically drew you to this organization? What do you hope to get out of this experience?                         |Genuine connection vs. “I need an internship for my program.” Both can be fine — but one shows mission fit.|
|**P2**|Tell me about a project — academic, volunteer, or personal — where you had to figure something out without much guidance.|Process, not outcome. How did they approach the unknown? Did they ask for help appropriately?              |
|**P3**|If I asked you to draft a one-paragraph email to our audience explaining what we do, what would you want to know first?  |Do they ask about audience, tone, purpose? Or do they jump to writing? Tests communication instinct.       |
|**P4**|What’s something you taught yourself recently — a skill, a tool, a topic? How did you go about it?                       |Learning process. Self-awareness about how they learn. Curiosity.                                          |
|—     |What does good feedback look like to you? How do you prefer to receive it?                                               |Coachability. Self-knowledge. This also sets the supervision relationship up well from Day 1.              |

### Interview Scoring

Same four criteria (P1–P4), scored 1–5. Add an Overall Impression row (/25 total).

**Decision:** Offer / Hold / Decline. For interns, a “Hold” typically becomes a decline unless the pool improves. Don’t hold a candidate hostage to your indecision.

-----

## Stage 3: Onboard (Week 1)

**Purpose:** Set the intern up to succeed and begin contributing quickly. Interns have less runway; a slow onboard wastes a third of a 12-week placement.

**Principle:** Everything an intern needs should fit on one page and be explained in one conversation.

### Day 1 Checklist

|Item                                                                    |✓|
|------------------------------------------------------------------------|-|
|Workspace and system access set up (email, shared drive, tools)         | |
|Introduction to supervisor and key people they’ll work with             | |
|Organization overview: mission, who we serve, how we operate            | |
|Internship scope document provided (see below)                          | |
|Meeting cadence established (weekly 1:1 with supervisor)                | |
|Communication norms explained (email, Slack, text, hours, response time)| |
|First assignment given — something achievable within 2–3 days           | |
|Exit presentation expectations explained (see Stage 5)                  | |

### Internship Scope Document (Template)

Hand this to the intern on Day 1. One page. It answers every question they’ll have in Week 1.

|Field                |Content                                                                          |
|---------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|**Intern Name**      |[Name]                                                                           |
|**Dates**            |[Start] – [End] ([X] weeks)                                                      |
|**Hours**            |[X] hours/week. Schedule: [days/times]                                           |
|**Supervisor**       |[Name], [contact info]                                                           |
|**Compensation**     |[Paid: $X/hr] or [Unpaid: academic credit through X program]                     |
|**Primary Project**  |1–2 sentence description of the main deliverable or focus area                   |
|**Secondary Tasks**  |Ongoing operational support tasks                                                |
|**Learning Goals**   |What the intern should be able to do or understand by the end that they can’t now|
|**Meeting Cadence**  |Weekly 1:1 with supervisor: [day/time]                                           |
|**Midpoint Check-in**|Week [X]: formal progress review                                                 |
|**Exit Presentation**|Final week: 15–20 min knowledge capture presentation (see Stage 5)               |
|**Key Contacts**     |[Names and roles of people the intern will interact with]                        |
|**Systems Access**   |[List of tools/platforms with login info location]                               |

## Stage 4: Manage (Ongoing + Midpoint)

**Purpose:** Keep the intern on track, developing, and contributing. Catch problems at the midpoint, not the exit.

### Weekly 1:1 (15–20 Minutes)

Same three questions every week. Takes five minutes to prep. Prevents drift.

1. **What did you work on this week?** (Accountability)
1. **What are you stuck on or confused about?** (Support)
1. **What’s your plan for next week?** (Direction)

**Supervisor’s job:** Listen, redirect if needed, remove obstacles, provide feedback. Do not do the intern’s work for them. The learning is in the struggle.

### Midpoint Check-in (Formal, 30 Minutes)

**When:** Halfway through the internship (Week 6 of a 12-week placement).

**Purpose:** Honest assessment. Course correct if needed. There’s still time to recover if something’s off track.

|      |Criteria                            |Score (1–5)|Evidence & Notes                                                                 |
|------|------------------------------------|-----------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|**M1**|Quality of Work Product             |           |Are deliverables meeting expectations? Improving over time?                      |
|**M2**|Reliability & Follow-Through        |           |Shows up on time, meets deadlines, does what they said they’d do?                |
|**M3**|Initiative & Problem-Solving        |           |Tries before asking? Asks good questions? Brings solutions, not just problems?   |
|**M4**|Coachability & Growth               |           |Receives feedback well? Applies it? Visibly improving?                           |
|**M5**|Organizational Fit & Professionalism|           |Works well with others? Represents the org appropriately? Understands boundaries?|
|      |**Total**                           |**/25**    |                                                                                 |

**Midpoint conversation framework:**

1. **Here’s what’s going well:** [specific examples]
1. **Here’s what I’d like to see more of:** [specific, actionable]
1. **Here’s what I need you to adjust:** [if applicable, direct but kind]
1. **What do you need from me for the second half?** [ask, then listen]

**Decision:** On Track / Needs Adjustment / Early Exit. If Early Exit, have the conversation promptly and with dignity. Lingering helps no one.

## Stage 5: Exit (Knowledge Capture & Presentation)

**The primary risk with interns is not bad performance — it’s knowledge loss.** The intern spent 8–16 weeks learning your systems, relationships, pain points, and workarounds. If they walk out the door without transferring that knowledge, the next person starts from zero.

This stage has three components: a written knowledge transfer document, a presentation to the team or relevant stakeholders, and a final evaluation.

### 5A: Knowledge Transfer Document

The intern completes this during their final week. It becomes a permanent organizational asset.

-----

**INTERN KNOWLEDGE TRANSFER DOCUMENT**

**Intern:** [Name]

**Dates:** [Start] – [End]

**Supervisor:** [Name]

**Primary Project:** [Title/Description]

-----

**1. What I Worked On**

List all projects, tasks, and ongoing responsibilities. For each, note:

- Current status (complete / in progress / not started)
- Where files live (specific folder paths or system locations)
- What the next person needs to know to pick this up

**2. What I Learned That Isn’t Written Down Anywhere**

This is the most valuable section. Document the informal knowledge:

- Workarounds you discovered (systems, processes, people)
- Unwritten rules or norms you figured out
- Relationships or contacts that were important and why
- Things that took you too long to figure out that could be documented for the next intern

**3. What’s Working Well**

Systems, processes, or practices that you saw functioning effectively. What should the org keep doing?

**4. What Could Be Better**

Honest, constructive observations. Interns see things that insiders have gone blind to. This is not a complaint box — it’s fresh-eyes feedback.

- Processes that are inefficient or confusing
- Tools that don’t work the way people think they do
- Gaps in documentation, training, or communication
- Things you wish you’d known on Day 1

**5. Recommendations for the Next Intern**

If someone starts this role tomorrow, what three things should they know? What should the org do differently to set them up better?

**6. Systems & Access Handoff**

- List all systems you had access to
- Confirm all passwords changed / access revoked on final day
- Note any files that need to be moved from personal to shared locations

-----

### 5B: Exit Presentation (15–20 Minutes)

**Purpose:** Transfer knowledge verbally, demonstrate growth, give the intern a professional presentation opportunity, and create a moment of closure for both sides.

**Audience:** Supervisor + anyone who worked with the intern. Keep it small enough that the intern feels comfortable, large enough that the knowledge reaches the people who need it.

**Format:** Simple slides or spoken presentation. The intern presents, not the supervisor. This is their work.

### Presentation Structure

|Slide|Title                        |Content                                                                                                        |
|-----|-----------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|1    |Title & Overview             |Name, role, dates, one-sentence summary of the internship focus                                                |
|2    |What I Did                   |Summary of projects and tasks. Concrete deliverables. Quantify where possible.                                 |
|3    |What I Learned               |Top 3–5 things learned — about the org, the sector, or themselves. This is where growth becomes visible.       |
|4    |What I Found                 |Fresh-eyes observations. What’s working. What could be improved. Specific, constructive, offered with humility.|
|5    |What I’d Tell the Next Intern|Practical advice for their successor. This slide becomes part of the onboarding materials for the next intern. |
|6    |Handoff & Status             |What’s complete, what’s in progress, where files live, who to contact for what. The operational handoff.       |
|7    |Thank You                    |Acknowledgments. Keep it brief and genuine.                                                                    |

**After the presentation:**

- Save the presentation and knowledge transfer document in a permanent, labeled location
- Both become part of the onboarding packet for the next intern
- Supervisor writes a brief assessment (see 5C below) and files it
- Revoke system access on the final day. Confirm with the intern that all personal files have been removed.

### 5C: Final Evaluation

|**F4**|Coachability & Growth               |           |                                                              |

## Legal & Compliance Note

**Paid vs. unpaid:** Under the Fair Labor Standards Act, for-profit employers must generally pay interns. Nonprofits have more latitude for unpaid volunteer interns, but the distinction between “intern” and “employee” still matters. If the intern is performing work that a paid employee would otherwise do, during set hours, under direct supervision, with no academic credit component — they should probably be paid.

**When in doubt:** Pay them. $15–20/hr for 10–15 hours/week is $1,500–$4,800 for a 10-week placement. That’s cheap compared to the FLSA exposure of getting it wrong.

**If unpaid:** Document the academic credit arrangement, ensure the intern’s school is involved, and confirm the internship primarily benefits the intern’s education, not the organization’s operations.

**Background checks:** Required if the intern will have access to vulnerable populations, financial systems, or confidential data. Check state requirements.
