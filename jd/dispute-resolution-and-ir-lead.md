## The Problem This Role Solves

Every dispute mechanism in HR — grievance procedures, EEOC charges, internal investigations — assumes a human made the decision. When AI is in the decision chain, three things break simultaneously:

|What Breaks                                                |Why It Matters                                         |What This Role Does                                                                    |
|-----------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|The employee may not know AI was involved                  |No basis to challenge the AI’s role                    |Builds disclosure infrastructure so AI involvement is known at the time of the decision|
|The responsible human may not understand what they approved|Cannot meaningfully defend a process they didn’t design|Builds genuine human review that is documented and real, not performative              |
|The documentation may not exist                            |No process can proceed                                 |Works with the Audit Analyst to establish documentation standards before disputes arise|


> *“The employee in front of you was affected by a decision a system made. Your job is to give them a human process — not a better chatbot.”*

## Core Responsibilities

### 1. AI Dispute Process Design and Operation

- Design and maintain the organizational process for employees to contest AI-assisted employment decisions
- Ensure the process is genuinely accessible — plain language, no technical prerequisites
- Run individual dispute proceedings: gather the AI decision record, review it against applicable standard, produce a finding, communicate the outcome
- Maintain the dispute register and review quarterly for systemic patterns

### 2. The Employee Experience in Dispute

- Sit across from an employee who feels wronged by a system they don’t understand — and run a process that is fair, comprehensible, and human
- Translate the AI decision record into plain language for the employee
- Ensure the employee can respond to the AI’s basis for the decision — not just appeal the outcome but challenge the inputs
- Provide a written finding in plain language

### 3. Labor Relations in AI Environments

- In unionized environments: advise on AI governance language for collective bargaining agreements before the first grievance files — disclosure obligations, human review requirements, appeal rights, prohibited AI uses in disciplinary proceedings
- Run AI-related grievance proceedings under existing CBAs where the agreement is silent or ambiguous
- In non-union environments: build dispute architecture providing AI-related appeal rights equivalent to what a well-designed CBA would provide
- Track emerging AI labor relations developments

### 4. Pre-Dispute Infrastructure

- Build the disclosure infrastructure: employees informed when AI was involved in a decision that affected them, at the time of the decision, in plain language
- Build the explanation infrastructure: employees receive a comprehensible account of how the decision was reached
- Partner with the Chain of Custody Lead to ensure documentation standards are in place before disputes arise
- Run periodic audits of AI-assisted decision processes for dispute vulnerability

### 5. Systemic Pattern Identification

- Review dispute register quarterly for patterns: clustering around specific AI tools, decision types, or demographic groups
- A pattern of individual disputes is a systemic problem
- Produce an annual AI dispute report

## The Union Environment: The Novel Territory

Most existing CBAs are silent on AI involvement in discipline, performance evaluation, and termination.

|What CBAs Typically Address                                       |What AI Creates That They Do Not                                               |
|------------------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|The standard of “just cause” for discipline                       |Whether AI-generated evidence meets just cause evidentiary standards           |
|The right to representation in investigatory interviews           |Whether AI-conducted intake conversations trigger Weingarten rights            |
|The requirement that discipline be based on documented performance|Whether AI-generated performance narratives constitute documentation           |
|Seniority and bumping rights in layoffs                           |Whether AI-assisted workforce reduction modeling is subject to seniority review|
|Grievance timelines                                               |When the timeline begins if the employee didn’t know AI was involved           |

The practitioner who fills this role builds the AI governance language for the next round of bargaining before the grievances that make it necessary have been filed.

## Qualifications

**Required:**

- Significant ER experience including direct management of formal dispute proceedings — grievance arbitration, EEOC charge response, internal investigations
- Working knowledge of AI system behavior in employment decision contexts
- Demonstrated ability to translate technical records into plain language
- Comfort in contested, adversarial environments while maintaining procedural integrity
- SPHR, SHRM-SCP, or equivalent

**Preferred:**

- Labor relations experience including collective bargaining and grievance arbitration
- Experience in EEOC charge response or employment litigation support
- Mediation training or certification
- Background in process design

**Not Required:**

- A legal degree — this role runs the process that employment counsel advises on
- Technical expertise in AI systems — the Audit Analyst provides that support
