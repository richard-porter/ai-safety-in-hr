# Change Management Reference Guide

## Kotter 8-Step Model Adapted for Resource-Constrained Organizations

*Governance Toolkit · Reference Series · Richard Porter · 2026*

*This is a reference tool, not a diagnostic instrument. Deploy when change is needed, not as ambient governance.*

-----

## Purpose

Most organizational change efforts fail not because the idea was wrong but because the implementation was. Boards approve new bylaws that nobody follows. Executives announce strategic pivots that volunteers ignore. Committees redesign processes that revert within six months.

John Kotter’s 8-Step Change Model, developed from research on organizational transformation at Harvard Business School, provides a sequenced framework for making change stick. The model was designed for large enterprises. This guide translates it for organizations where the “workforce” is mostly volunteers, the “budget” is mostly donated or constrained, and the “board” meets monthly for ninety minutes.

**When to use:** Any time an organization is implementing a change that affects how people work, not just what the board approved. Examples: new bylaws, restructured committees, leadership transitions, program launches or closures, governance model shifts, merger or affiliation, technology adoption.

**When not to use:** Routine operational adjustments that don’t change roles, relationships, or expectations. Changing the meeting time is not a change management event. Changing the meeting structure is.

-----

## The Model: Kotter’s 8 Steps

Kotter’s model is sequential. Steps 1–4 build the foundation. Steps 5–7 execute the change. Step 8 locks it in. Skipping steps is the primary cause of change failure. The most commonly skipped steps in small organizations are 1 (urgency) and 8 (anchoring).

|#|Step                         |Enterprise Version                                      |Resource-Constrained Translation                                                                                 |
|-|-----------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|1|**Create Urgency**           |Market data, competitive threat, burning platform       |Mission gap, community need, funder expectation, regulatory change, volunteer attrition                          |
|2|**Build a Guiding Coalition**|Senior leadership team, executive sponsors              |Board champion + executive + 1–2 respected volunteers or staff. Not a committee — a coalition.                   |
|3|**Form a Strategic Vision**  |Vision statement, strategic plan, transformation roadmap|One sentence: “After this change, we will be able to _____ that we cannot do today.”                             |
|4|**Communicate the Vision**   |Town halls, email campaigns, leadership messaging       |Board meeting presentation + one-page summary. Say it 7 times in 7 ways.                                         |
|5|**Empower Action**           |Remove barriers, realign systems, retrain workforce     |Remove the specific obstacle blocking adoption. Usually: unclear roles, missing tools, or one resistant person.  |
|6|**Generate Quick Wins**      |90-day targets, pilot programs, visible milestones      |First visible result within 30–60 days. Not the whole change — a piece of it people can see and feel.            |
|7|**Sustain Acceleration**     |Build on wins, expand scope, deepen transformation      |Don’t declare victory after the quick win. Use the momentum to tackle the next piece. Keep the coalition visible.|
|8|**Anchor in Culture**        |Update policies, reward systems, hiring criteria        |Write it into bylaws, onboarding, or annual calendar. If it’s not in a document, it’s not anchored.              |

## Step-by-Step Translation

### Step 1: Create Urgency

**The resource-constrained version:** “We are failing the people we exist to serve, and here is the evidence.”

Urgency is the hardest step in a volunteer-driven organization because nobody’s getting fired if things stay the same. Without urgency, change is optional, and optional change doesn’t happen.

**How to create urgency without a burning platform:**

- **Data:** “We served 200 fewer families this year than last year.” Numbers create urgency that opinions cannot.
- **Story:** One specific person, one specific moment where the gap was visible. Data plus story is more powerful than either alone.
- **External pressure:** A funder requirement, a regulatory change, a community partner’s expectation. “We need to do this” is weaker than “this is happening whether we act or not.”
- **Departure data:** If your People Pulse shows declining engagement or increasing departure intent, that is urgency data. Use it.

**The mistake:** Skipping urgency and going straight to the solution. People resist solutions to problems they don’t believe exist.

-----

### Step 2: Build a Guiding Coalition

**The resource-constrained version:** Three to five people who are respected, committed, and willing to be publicly associated with the change.

A coalition is not a committee. A committee has a charter, meets on a schedule, and produces minutes. A coalition has a mission, meets when needed, and produces results.

**Selection criteria:**

- **Position power:** Can they authorize resources or decisions?
- **Expertise:** Do they understand the domain being changed?
- **Credibility:** Do people trust them?
- **Leadership:** Will they advocate publicly, not just agree privately? (This is the filter that matters most.)

**The mistake:** Building the coalition from people who agree with you instead of people who are respected by the organization. A coalition of allies is a clique. A coalition of influencers is a force.

-----

### Step 3: Form a Strategic Vision

**The resource-constrained version:** One sentence that anyone can repeat from memory.

The vision is not the plan. The vision is the destination. The plan is the route.

**The test:** Can someone who missed the last two board meetings explain the change in one sentence? If not, the vision isn’t clear enough.

**Formula:** *“After this change, we will be able to [specific capability] that we cannot do today.”*

Examples:

- “After this change, every new volunteer will know their role, their supervisor, and their first task before they arrive.”
- “After this change, the board will receive financial reports they can actually read and act on.”
- “After this change, we will be able to serve families in the eastern district without relying on a single volunteer’s personal vehicle.”

**The mistake:** Confusing the mechanism with the vision. “We’re implementing a new volunteer management system” is a mechanism. “Every volunteer will feel prepared and supported” is a vision.

-----

### Step 4: Communicate the Vision

**The resource-constrained version:** Say it seven times in seven ways. Then say it again.

Communication is not a one-time announcement. It is repetition across channels until the message becomes ambient.

**The 7×7 Rule:**

1. Board meeting presentation (formal, with data)
1. One-page summary (printed, handed out, posted on the wall)
1. Email to all volunteers/staff (personal tone, from the executive or Board Chair)
1. One-on-one conversations with key influencers (coalition members lead these)
1. Agenda item at the next volunteer gathering (brief, Q&A invited)
1. Public-facing communication (newsletter, social media — mission-connected)
1. Follow-up at the next board meeting (progress update, not re-announcement)

**The mistake:** Announcing the change once and assuming everyone heard it, understood it, and accepted it. Communication is not information transfer. It is meaning-making, and meaning-making takes repetition.

-----

### Step 5: Empower Action

**The resource-constrained version:** Find the one thing blocking adoption and remove it.

In a small organization, the barrier is usually one of three things:

- **Role confusion:** People don’t know who’s responsible for what under the new system. Fix: publish a RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) for the changed process.
- **Missing tools:** The new process requires something people don’t have — a form, a login, a template, a contact list. Fix: provide the tool before asking for the behavior.
- **One resistant person:** Often a long-tenured volunteer or board member with informal veto power through passive resistance. Fix: direct conversation (coalition member, not executive), listen to concerns, offer a role in the new system.

**The mistake:** Asking people to change behavior without changing the environment that supports the old behavior. If the old sign-up sheet is still on the wall, people will use the old sign-up sheet.

-----

### Step 6: Generate Quick Wins

**The resource-constrained version:** One visible result within 30–60 days that people can point to and say “that’s different.”

Quick wins are not about proving the entire change works. They are about proving the change is real.

**Characteristics of a good quick win:**

- **Visible:** People who weren’t involved can see the result without being told about it.
- **Unambiguous:** It clearly connects to the change vision, not to business-as-usual.
- **Low-cost:** It didn’t require heroic effort or special resources.
- **Attributable:** People can see which action produced the result.

**The mistake:** Waiting for the whole change to be complete before showing results. Six months of invisible implementation breeds the assumption that nothing is happening.

-----

### Step 7: Sustain Acceleration

**The resource-constrained version:** Don’t stop after the quick win. Use the credibility to tackle the next piece.

This is where most small organization change efforts die. The quick win creates a sense of completion. The coalition relaxes. The board moves on.

**How to sustain:**

- **Name the next milestone:** Before celebrating the quick win, announce what comes next.
- **Keep the coalition visible:** The people who championed the change should continue to be publicly associated with it.
- **Report progress:** Monthly or quarterly, brief, to the board and to all participants. Not a presentation — a scoreboard.
- **Adjust:** Use People Pulse and Stay Interview data to identify what’s working and what isn’t. Adjust the approach, not the vision.

**The mistake:** Declaring victory. Kotter’s research is unambiguous: premature celebration is the single most common cause of change reversal. The change is not done when the policy is approved. It is done when the behavior has become the default.

-----

### Step 8: Anchor in Culture

**The resource-constrained version:** Write it down. Put it in a document that outlives the current leadership.

Culture in a small organization is whatever the current executive and Board Chair do. If the change depends on a specific person’s energy, it will die when that person leaves.

**Anchoring checklist:**

- Is the change reflected in the bylaws or policy manual? If it should be, update them.
- Is the change part of new volunteer/staff onboarding? If not, add it.
- Is the change on the annual calendar?
- Would a new executive or Board Chair know this change exists without being told? If not, document it.
- Has the old process been formally retired? (Old forms removed, old procedures marked obsolete.)

**The mistake:** Assuming that because people are doing the new thing now, they’ll keep doing it. Behavior follows structure. If the structure doesn’t change, the behavior will revert.

## Common Pitfalls

|Pitfall                     |What It Looks Like                                                                                                                                    |
|----------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|**The Volunteer Veto**      |A long-tenured volunteer passively resists by not showing up, not using the new process, or telling newer volunteers “we don’t really do it that way.”|
|**The Board Shrug**         |Board approves the change unanimously, then never asks about it again. The change dies of neglect.                                                    |
|**The Executive Bottleneck**|All change energy flows through the executive. When the executive is unavailable, the change stalls. No coalition means no distributed ownership.     |
|**The Perfect Plan Trap**   |Months spent designing the ideal new system. By the time it’s ready, the urgency has dissipated and the coalition has moved on.                       |
|**The Premature Victory**   |The first quick win is celebrated as the completion of the change. Six months later, everything has reverted.                                         |
|**The Unfired Predecessor** |The old process or form is never formally retired. People who prefer the old way use its continued existence as justification for not changing.       |

## Toolkit Connections

- **People Pulse:** Pulse Check data provides urgency evidence (Step 1). Stay Interview data identifies barriers (Step 5). Departure Debrief data reveals whether change resistance caused attrition (Step 7).
- **Recruiter Tool:** New hires and volunteers onboarded during a change initiative need the change vision incorporated into their onboarding (Step 8 anchoring).
- **Intern Tool:** Knowledge capture at exit should include documentation of changed processes to prevent institutional memory from defaulting to pre-change procedures.
- **Governance Tools:** Bylaw changes, committee restructuring, and governance model shifts are all change management events that benefit from the Kotter sequence.
- **Merger & Affiliation Readiness Assessment:** A merger or affiliation is a major change event. After the readiness assessment, use the Kotter framework to plan and execute the integration.
- **Negative Space Mapper / Talent Calibration Grid:** Diagnostic findings that reveal governance gaps create natural urgency (Step 1) and identify coalition candidates (Step 2).

-----

*Change fails when leaders treat it as an event instead of a process. Kotter’s model works because it treats change as a sequence of human problems, not a technical implementation. In an organization where every participant is there by choice, the human problems are the only problems.*
