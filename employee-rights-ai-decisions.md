# Your Rights When AI Is Involved in a Decision About You

*Plain language. No jargon. What you need to know.*

*Part of the AI Safety in HR Framework · 2026*

-----

> If a decision at work affected you — and you think AI may have been involved — you have rights. This document explains what they are and what to do with them. You do not need technical knowledge to use them.

-----

## What Counts as an AI-Assisted Decision?

You may have encountered AI involvement in any of these:

|Process                      |What AI May Have Done                                                                                  |
|-----------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|**Hiring**                   |Your application may have been screened, ranked, or evaluated by AI before a human reviewed it         |
|**Performance reviews**      |Your rating or review narrative may have been generated or influenced by AI                            |
|**Compensation**             |Your pay band or merit recommendation may have been produced by AI                                     |
|**Benefits**                 |Your eligibility determination — including claims decisions — may have been made by an automated system|
|**Discipline or termination**|Documentation supporting a disciplinary action may have been generated or influenced by AI             |
|**Workforce reductions**     |Selection for layoff may have involved AI-assisted workforce modeling                                  |

If you are not sure whether AI was involved, you have the right to ask.

-----

## Your Right to Know

You have the right to know when AI was involved in a decision that affected your employment.

**How to ask:**

> *“Was AI involved in this decision? If so, what tool was used, what data did it use, and who reviewed its output before the decision was made?”*

Put this in writing — email is fine. Keep a copy.

-----

## Your Right to Contest

If you believe a decision was wrong — regardless of whether AI was involved — you have the right to contest it. When AI was involved, you have additional grounds:

**Challenge the inputs.** If the AI used incorrect data about you, that is a basis for review. AI systems make errors. They also use proxy variables — patterns correlated with protected characteristics like race, age, or pregnancy status — that can produce discriminatory outcomes without anyone intending it.

**Challenge the human review.** If a human approved AI output without genuinely reviewing it — rubber-stamping an output in a queue of hundreds — the decision may not meet the required standard of care.

**Request the documentation.** You are entitled to see the record that supported the decision — including what the AI produced and what the human did with it.

**ERISA appeal rights.** If a benefits determination was made by an automated system, you have a statutory right to a full and fair review by a human reviewer. This right exists regardless of what the system says about its own finality.

-----

## Your Right to a Human Process

> **No AI system can make a final employment decision about you without a human being accountable for it. If you cannot identify the human who reviewed and authorized a decision that affected you — ask.**

In practice:

- Any appeal of an AI-assisted decision must be reviewed by a human — not the same AI system or a different one
- You have the right to communicate directly with a human HR representative about any decision that significantly affects your employment
- If you are in a unionized environment, your collective bargaining agreement may provide additional rights — speak with your union representative

-----

## What to Do If You Have a Concern

**Step 1: Ask for the record.**
Request a plain-language explanation of how the decision was made, including what AI tools were involved, what data they used, and who reviewed the output.

**Step 2: Document your request.**
Put it in writing — email is fine. Note the date. Keep a copy. This creates a record that you asked.

**Step 3: Use the appeal process.**
Ask your HR representative what the formal appeal path is. Every employment decision that significantly affects you — termination, discipline, demotion, significant pay change — should have one.

**Step 4: Escalate if needed.**
To HR leadership, to your union representative if applicable, or — for benefits denials — directly to the plan’s claims administrator under ERISA.

**Step 5: If you believe you have been subjected to illegal discrimination**
through an AI-assisted process, you may file a charge with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) at eeoc.gov or with your state’s civil rights agency. You do not need a lawyer to file a charge. There are time limits — generally 180 or 300 days from the discriminatory act — so do not wait.

## Organizational Commitments

When this framework is in place, these are the standards your organization has committed to:

|Commitment        |What It Means for You                                                                                                                              |
|------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|**Disclosure**    |You will be told when AI was involved in a decision that affected you                                                                              |
|**Transparency**  |You can get a plain-language explanation of what the AI did and what data it used                                                                  |
|**Human review**  |Every AI-assisted employment decision has a named human who reviewed and authorized it                                                             |
|**Genuine appeal**|Your contest will be reviewed by a human who was not involved in the original decision                                                             |
|**Documentation** |The record of AI involvement in decisions that affect you is maintained and available on request                                                   |
|**Accommodation** |If you need an accommodation to participate in an AI-assisted process, you have the right to request one and to have your request routed to a human|

-----

## A Note on Benefits Denials

If an AI system denied a benefits claim — health, disability, leave, or other plan benefit — and you believe the denial was wrong, your rights are stronger than in most other employment contexts. ERISA (the Employee Retirement Income Security Act) requires:

- A written explanation of why your claim was denied, in plain language
- The specific plan provisions on which the denial was based
- A description of what additional information, if any, could complete your claim
- An explanation of the plan’s review procedures

An AI-generated denial letter that does not include these elements may not satisfy ERISA’s requirements, regardless of whether the underlying determination was correct.
