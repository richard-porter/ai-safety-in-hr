# Policy Governance Reference Guide

## Carver Model Translated for Small and Mid-Size Boards

*Governance Toolkit · Reference Series*

*Richard Porter · 2026*

*This is a reference tool, not an implementation manual. It translates the principles of John Carver’s Policy Governance model into language and structures that any small organization’s board can adopt incrementally — nonprofit, association, cooperative, or community entity. Full Carver implementation typically requires board training and consultant support. This guide provides the conceptual foundation and practical starting points.*

-----

## The Problem This Model Solves

Most small organization boards operate in one of two failure modes:

**Failure Mode 1: Micromanagement.** The board involves itself in operational details — approving vendor selections, reviewing individual personnel decisions, debating the color of the annual report. The ED or executive feels undermined. The board feels busy but ineffective. Meetings run long and accomplish little of strategic value.

**Failure Mode 2: Rubber-Stamping.** The board defers entirely to the executive, approving whatever is placed in front of it without meaningful scrutiny. The executive feels unsupported. The board feels uninformed. Governance exists on paper but not in practice.

Both failure modes share the same root cause: the board and the executive have never clearly defined what belongs to whom. Without that boundary, every issue becomes a negotiation. Who decides the budget format? Who approves a new program? Who handles a volunteer complaint? When these questions are answered ad hoc, the answer depends on personalities rather than principles.

John Carver’s Policy Governance model solves this by making the boundary explicit, principled, and documented. The board owns the “what” and the “why.” The executive owns the “how.” And the boundary is defined not by tradition or personality but by written policy.

-----

## Core Concepts

Carver’s model rests on four policy categories. Everything a board does falls into one of these categories. If a board activity doesn’t fit any category, the board probably shouldn’t be doing it.

|Policy Category            |What It Covers                                                                                                                              |Who Owns It                                                                                                                           |
|---------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|**Ends**                   |What benefit the organization produces, for whom, and at what cost or relative priority. The mission translated into measurable outcomes.   |The board defines Ends. This is the board’s primary job. Everything else is secondary.                                                |
|**Executive Limitations**  |What the executive may NOT do in pursuing the Ends. Boundaries on methods, conduct, and risk. Defined negatively: “the executive shall not…”|The board sets the boundaries. The executive operates freely within them. Any reasonable interpretation of the boundary is acceptable.|
|**Board–Executive Linkage**|How authority is delegated, how the executive reports, how the board monitors compliance. The relationship contract.                        |The board defines the linkage. The executive operates under it.                                                                       |
|**Governance Process**     |How the board itself operates: its own discipline, structure, meeting conduct, officer roles, committee usage, and self-evaluation.         |The board governs itself. This is the board’s internal operating system.                                                              |

-----

## The Big Idea: Ends vs. Means

The single most important concept in Carver’s model is the distinction between Ends and Means. Most board dysfunction traces back to a board that is managing Means instead of governing Ends.

|Ends (Board’s Job)                                                                     |Means (Executive’s Job)                                                        |
|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|What outcomes should our community experience because this organization exists?        |What programs, processes, staffing, and operations will produce those outcomes?|
|“Children in our county will have warm coats for winter.”                              |How coats are collected, stored, sorted, distributed, and tracked.             |
|“Low-income families will have access to nutritious food within 5 miles of their home.”|Where the pantry is located, what hours it operates, how inventory is managed. |
|Defined by the board in broad, outcome-focused language.                               |Determined by the executive using any reasonable method.                       |
|Monitored by the board through reporting and data.                                     |Managed by the executive without board involvement in operational details.     |

**The test:** If the board is debating how something gets done, it has crossed into Means. The board’s question should always be: “Are we achieving the outcomes we defined?” not “Are we doing the work the way I would do it?”

**The hardest discipline:** Board members who are subject matter experts (former teachers on an education board, healthcare professionals on a clinic board, accountants on a financial literacy board) will naturally gravitate toward Means because they have opinions about how the work should be done. Carver requires them to set those opinions aside unless the executive’s methods violate an Executive Limitation. Expertise informs the boundary-setting, not the operations.

-----

## Executive Limitations: The Negative Boundary

This is the concept that most boards find counterintuitive. Instead of telling the executive what to do (which is micromanagement), the board tells the executive what NOT to do (which is boundary-setting). The executive is free to use any reasonable method to achieve the Ends, as long as no Executive Limitation is violated.

**The principle:** Don’t prescribe. Proscribe.

|Instead of (Prescriptive)                                                                      |Use (Proscriptive / Limitation)                                                                                                                                |
|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|“The executive shall submit a detailed budget to the Finance Committee for line-item approval.”|“The executive shall not allow budgeting that deviates materially from board-stated priorities, risks fiscal jeopardy, or fails to maintain adequate reserves.”|
|“The executive shall use competitive bidding for purchases over $500.”                         |“The executive shall not make purchases that are imprudent, fail to receive comparable pricing, or create conflicts of interest.”                              |
|“The executive shall hold monthly staff meetings.”                                             |“The executive shall not allow staff to be uninformed about organizational direction or to lack a mechanism for raising concerns.”                             |
|“The executive shall submit quarterly program reports.”                                        |“The executive shall not allow the board to be uninformed about Ends achievement, fiscal condition, or material risks.”                                        |

**Why negative framing works:** It gives the executive maximum operational flexibility while maintaining clear boundaries. A prescriptive policy (“submit quarterly reports”) becomes a compliance exercise. A proscriptive policy (“don’t let the board be uninformed”) creates accountability for outcomes.

**“Any reasonable interpretation”:** This is the phrase that makes Carver work and that most boards struggle with. If the executive’s interpretation of a Limitation is reasonable — even if a board member would have chosen a different approach — the executive is in compliance. The board’s remedy is to narrow the Limitation in the next policy review, not to override the executive’s current decision.

-----

## Monitoring: How the Board Stays Informed Without Managing

If the board defines Ends and sets Executive Limitations but never checks whether they’re being achieved and respected, the model collapses into rubber-stamping. Monitoring is the mechanism that keeps governance active without crossing into management.

|Method               |What It Is                                                                                                                                  |Application                                                                                                                                                                                                                                            |
|---------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|**Internal Report**  |Executive reports to the board on compliance with a specific Ends or Limitation policy. The executive provides evidence, not just assertion.|The board’s job is to determine whether the evidence demonstrates a reasonable interpretation of the policy — not to instruct how to fix it.                                                                                                           |
|**External Audit**   |An independent party evaluates compliance. The classic example is the financial audit.                                                      |Annual financial audit. Can also include program evaluation by an external reviewer.                                                                                                                                                                   |
|**Direct Inspection**|Board members observe operations firsthand. Used sparingly and by prior arrangement.                                                        |A board member visits a program site, attends an orientation, or reviews a process in person. This is a scheduled, transparent observation — not a surprise inspection. Used only when internal reports and external audits leave questions unanswered.|

**The monitoring calendar:** Each Ends policy and Executive Limitation should be monitored on a schedule — typically annually, with some (financial conditions, risk) monitored quarterly. The board should maintain a monitoring calendar so that every policy is reviewed at least once per year.

**The monitoring question:** When reviewing an executive’s internal report, the board asks one question: “Does this evidence demonstrate a reasonable interpretation of our policy?” If yes, the executive is in compliance. If no, the board discusses whether to accept the interpretation, request additional evidence, or revise the policy. The board does not instruct the executive on how to fix the issue — that would be managing Means.

-----

## Board–Executive Linkage: The Relationship Contract

The Board–Executive Linkage policies define how authority flows between the board and the executive. This is where most informal governance breaks down, because the relationship is usually defined by personality and habit rather than by policy.

**Key linkage principles:**

- **The board speaks with one voice.** Individual board members have no authority over the executive. Only the board as a whole — through formal action — can direct, evaluate, or constrain the executive. A board member who privately tells the executive to do something is operating outside the governance model.
- **The executive reports to the board, not to individual members.** The Board Chair may serve as the primary communication channel, but the Chair has no more authority than any other board member outside of meetings. The Chair facilitates; the board decides.
- **Delegation is to the executive, not through the executive.** The board delegates authority to one person: the executive director. The executive then organizes staff, volunteers, and operations as they see fit. The board does not delegate to staff members directly or create reporting relationships that bypass the executive.
- **Evaluation is against policy, not personality.** The executive’s performance is evaluated by one criterion: are the Ends being achieved within the Executive Limitations? Not: “Do we like the executive?” Not: “Does the executive run meetings the way I would?” The question is always: outcomes achieved, boundaries respected.

**The hardest conversation:** When a board member disagrees with the executive’s operational choices but the executive is in compliance with all policies, the board member has two options: propose a new or revised Limitation, or accept the executive’s reasonable interpretation. There is no third option. “I don’t like how you’re doing it” is not a governance concern unless it violates a stated boundary.

-----

## Governance Process: The Board’s Own Discipline

The fourth policy category governs the board itself. This is the self-regulation component: how the board conducts its own business, maintains its own discipline, and holds itself accountable for its own performance.

**Key governance process elements:**

- **Board job description:** The board’s job is to define Ends, set Executive Limitations, monitor compliance, maintain linkage with the community, and govern itself. That’s the complete list. Everything else is either the executive’s job or nobody’s job.
- **Meeting discipline:** Board meetings should be primarily devoted to Ends discussion, monitoring reports, and policy revision. If the majority of meeting time is spent on operational updates, the board is managing, not governing.
- **Committee usage:** In Carver’s model, committees exist to help the board do its job, not to help the executive do theirs. A “Fundraising Committee” that plans events is an operational committee — it does the executive’s work. A “Governance Committee” that reviews board policies is a governance committee — it does the board’s work. Carver recommends minimizing committees and eliminating operational ones entirely.
- **Board self-evaluation:** The board should evaluate its own performance annually, using its own Governance Process policies as the standard. Are we spending our time on Ends? Are we monitoring effectively? Are we maintaining our own discipline? If the board evaluates the executive but never evaluates itself, governance is one-directional.

## Adopting Carver in a Small Organization

Full Carver implementation requires a complete policy manual covering all four categories, board training, and ongoing discipline. Most small boards cannot do this all at once. The following is an incremental adoption path.

### Phase 1: Clarify the Boundary (Months 1–3)

The single highest-value action: write down the Ends/Means boundary for your organization. This does not require a full policy manual. It requires one conversation and one document.

**Exercise: The Boundary Conversation**

At a board meeting, distribute this list and ask each board member to mark each item as **B** (Board decides) or **E** (Executive decides):

- Setting the annual budget total
- Deciding budget line-item allocations
- Hiring the executive director
- Hiring staff below the executive
- Choosing program locations
- Setting volunteer policies
- Approving vendor contracts over $1,000
- Determining which communities to serve
- Selecting the annual audit firm
- Designing the volunteer onboarding process
- Setting the organization’s strategic priorities
- Deciding how strategic priorities are implemented

Where there is disagreement, you have found the boundary ambiguity that is causing governance friction. Resolve each item by asking: “Is this about WHAT we should achieve (Ends) or HOW we achieve it (Means)?” Document the results. That document is the beginning of your governance framework.

### Phase 2: Write Three Executive Limitations (Months 3–6)

You do not need 20 Executive Limitation policies. Start with three that address your organization’s most common boundary violations:

**Limitation 1: Financial Condition**
*“The executive shall not allow the organization to operate without adequate financial reserves, fail to maintain accurate financial records, or make expenditures that materially deviate from board-approved budget priorities.”*

**Limitation 2: Treatment of People**
*“The executive shall not allow conditions that are unsafe, disrespectful, or that deny individuals their rights. This applies to staff, volunteers, clients, and community members.”*

**Limitation 3: Board Information**
*“The executive shall not allow the board to be uninformed about the organization’s financial condition, material risks, Ends achievement, or compliance with Executive Limitations.”*

These three Limitations cover the majority of governance concerns in a small organization. They can be expanded and refined over time. The act of writing them down — and agreeing on them as a board — is more valuable than the specific language.

### Phase 3: Implement Monitoring (Months 6–12)

|Quarter|Monitoring Focus                                                     |Method                                                                                                      |
|-------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|**Q1** |Financial Condition (Limitation 1)                                   |Internal report from executive + external audit results if available                                        |
|**Q2** |Ends Achievement                                                     |Internal report from executive with outcome data                                                            |
|**Q3** |Treatment of People (Limitation 2) + Board Information (Limitation 3)|Internal report from executive + board self-evaluation                                                      |
|**Q4** |Annual review of all policies + executive evaluation                 |Board reviews all policies for needed revision. Executive evaluation based on Ends + Limitations compliance.|

### Phase 4: Refine and Expand (Year 2+)

After one full year of operating with the basic framework, the board will have enough experience to identify where additional Limitations are needed, where Ends statements need refinement, and where the monitoring process needs adjustment. This is the time to consider additional policy development, board training, or consultant engagement.

**The principle:** Adopt the concept before the infrastructure. A board that understands Ends vs. Means and operates with three clear Limitations is governing better than a board with a 50-page Carver policy manual it doesn’t follow.

## Common Pitfalls in Carver Adoption

|Pitfall                                      |What It Looks Like                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                           |
|---------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|**The Policy Manual Fantasy**                |The board spends six months writing a comprehensive Carver policy manual, adopts it unanimously, and then continues operating exactly as before. The manual sits in a binder. Nobody references it.                                                                                          |
|**The “Any Reasonable Interpretation” Panic**|A board member discovers the executive made a decision they disagree with but that falls within a reasonable interpretation of the Limitation. Instead of accepting the model, they push to add prescriptive specificity to every policy, undermining the flexibility that makes Carver work.|
|**The Ends Avoidance**                       |The board writes detailed Executive Limitations and Governance Process policies but never defines its Ends. This is governing the how without deciding the what. It’s the most comfortable form of governance failure because it feels productive.                                           |
|**The Lone Board Member**                    |One board member “adopts Carver” and begins correcting other members in meetings: “That’s a Means question, we shouldn’t be discussing it.” Without full board buy-in, this creates resentment rather than governance improvement.                                                           |
|**The Executive Without a Net**              |The board adopts Carver’s delegation model but fails to implement monitoring. The executive has broad authority with no accountability mechanism. This is not Carver — it is abdication with theoretical justification.                                                                      |
|**The Committee Problem**                    |The board eliminates all committees per Carver’s recommendation but provides no alternative mechanism for board members to contribute between meetings. Board engagement plummets.                                                                                                           |

## Carver vs. Traditional Governance: A Comparison

|Dimension                |Traditional                                                                             |Carver                                                                      |
|-------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|**Board’s primary job**  |Approve, review, and advise on operational matters                                      |Define Ends, set Limitations, monitor, and self-govern                      |
|**Executive’s authority**|Permission-based: executive proposes, board approves                                    |Boundary-based: executive acts freely within stated Limitations             |
|**Budget approval**      |Board reviews and approves line items                                                   |Board sets fiscal boundaries; executive manages within them                 |
|**Committees**           |Operational committees (Finance, Program, Fundraising) that parallel executive functions|Governance committees only; operational work belongs to the executive       |
|**Executive evaluation** |Subjective: personality, relationship, board satisfaction                               |Objective: Ends achieved within Limitations                                 |
|**Meeting content**      |Operational updates, committee reports, approval votes                                  |Ends discussion, monitoring reports, policy review                          |
|**Risk**                 |Micromanagement or rubber-stamping, depending on board personality                      |Requires discipline; can feel abstract; may frustrate hands-on board members|

## Should Your Board Adopt Carver?

Carver is not the right model for every organization. Use this decision guide honestly:

**Carver is a good fit if:**

- Your board regularly debates operational details that the executive should control
- Your executive feels micromanaged or unclear about their authority
- Board meetings are consumed by reports and approvals rather than strategy
- You have a competent executive who would thrive with more autonomy and clear boundaries
- Your board is willing to invest in understanding a new governance model

**Carver is a poor fit if:**

- Your organization has no paid executive (all-volunteer organizations need a different model)
- Your executive is new, untested, or struggling — broad delegation requires earned trust
- Your board is not willing to spend meeting time on governance process rather than program details
- Your organization is in crisis — Carver is a steady-state model, not a turnaround tool
- Your board members joined specifically to “do the work” and will resist a governance-only role

**The hybrid option:** Many small organizations adopt Carver principles without full implementation. They write an Ends statement, create three to five Executive Limitations, implement a monitoring calendar, and continue using some operational committees with clearer boundaries. This is not “pure Carver” but it addresses the most common governance failures without requiring complete transformation. Be honest about what you are doing: “We are using Carver-informed governance” rather than “We are a Carver board.”

## Toolkit Connections

- **Change Management (Kotter):** Adopting Carver IS a change management event. Use the Kotter 8-Step sequence: create urgency (governance failure data), build a coalition (board champions), form a vision (“the board that governs, not manages”), communicate, empower, generate quick wins (first monitoring report), sustain, and anchor in bylaws.
- **Governance Audit:** The audit’s Mission/Ends and Board–Executive Relationship domains map directly to Carver’s Ends and Board–Executive Linkage policies. If Carver is adopted, the audit standards can be updated to reflect Carver-specific language.
- **People Pulse:** Engagement data from staff and volunteers feeds directly into Ends monitoring (are we serving people effectively?) and Executive Limitation monitoring (is the executive maintaining appropriate working conditions?).
- **Annual Board Report:** Under Carver, the annual report should be structured around Ends achievement and Limitation compliance, not operational activity.
- **Negative Space Mapper:** The Mapper identifies governance gaps. Carver provides the framework for filling them.

-----

*Carver’s model asks boards to do the hardest thing in organizational life: focus on what matters most and leave the rest to someone else. Most boards would rather be busy than strategic, because busy feels like contribution and strategic feels like abstraction. The discipline of Ends over Means is the discipline of trusting the people you hired to do the work — and holding them accountable for outcomes, not methods.*

*The board that governs well is the board that resists the urge to manage.*
